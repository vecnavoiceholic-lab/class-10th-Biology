Q1Hard
A person suffers damage to the villi of the small intestine. Which process will be affected the most?
ADigestion of proteins
BAbsorption of nutrients
CEmulsification of fats
DSecretion of digestive enzymes
Correct Answer
B — Absorption of nutrients. Villi are finger-like projections that massively increase surface area for nutrient absorption. Damage reduces this surface area, directly impairing absorption.
Q2Hard
Which statement correctly explains why the left ventricle has the thickest muscular wall?
AIt pumps blood to the lungs.
BIt receives oxygenated blood from the lungs.
CIt pumps blood to the entire body against high pressure.
DIt prevents mixing of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood.
Correct Answer
C — Pumps blood to the entire body against high pressure. Systemic circulation demands far greater force than pulmonary circulation, hence thicker walls.
Q3Hard
During vigorous exercise, the concentration of carbon dioxide in blood increases. This directly causes:
AFaster heartbeat only
BFaster breathing rate only
CIncreased breathing rate and heartbeat
DReduced breathing rate
Correct Answer
C — Increased breathing rate and heartbeat. Elevated CO₂ is sensed by the brain's respiratory centre, triggering both faster breathing (to expel CO₂) and a faster heart rate (to deliver more O₂).
Q4Hard
Which of the following would immediately stop ATP production in human cells?
AAbsence of saliva
BAbsence of oxygen and glucose together
CAbsence of bile juice
DAbsence of hydrochloric acid
Correct Answer
B — Absence of oxygen and glucose together. Glucose is the primary fuel; oxygen is required for aerobic respiration. Without both, all ATP-generating pathways (aerobic and anaerobic) cease.
Q5Hard
A plant kept in a humid environment shows reduced transpiration. Which process is most directly affected?
AWater absorption by roots
BUpward movement of water in xylem
CPhotosynthesis
DGermination
Correct Answer
B — Upward movement of water in xylem. Transpiration creates the pulling force (transpiration pull) that drives water up through the xylem. Less transpiration = weaker pull = slower ascent.
Q6Hard
Which structure prevents the backflow of blood into the left atrium?
ASemilunar valve
BTricuspid valve
CBicuspid valve
DSeptum
Correct Answer
C — Bicuspid (Mitral) valve. Located between the left atrium and left ventricle, it prevents backflow into the atrium when the ventricle contracts.
Q7Hard
Which event occurs first after food enters the stomach?
AAbsorption of amino acids
BAction of pepsin on proteins
CNeutralisation of acid by bile
DReabsorption of water
Correct Answer
B — Action of pepsin on proteins. The stomach secretes pepsin (activated by HCl) which begins protein digestion immediately. Bile, amino acid absorption, and water reabsorption occur later in the intestine.
Q8Hard
A plant loses large quantities of water but continues to absorb water from soil. This is mainly because:
ARoot pressure alone is sufficient
BTranspiration pull continuously operates
CXylem actively pumps water upward
DPhloem transports water upward
Correct Answer
B — Transpiration pull continuously operates. The suction created by evaporation from leaves draws water upward through xylem, which in turn pulls water in from the soil through roots.
Q9Very Hard
If the kidneys fail to remove urea effectively, which system will be affected first?
ADigestive system
BRespiratory system
CInternal chemical balance of the body
DSkeletal system
Correct Answer
C — Internal chemical balance (homeostasis). Urea accumulation causes uremia, disrupting blood chemistry, pH, and osmotic balance — the body's homeostasis is compromised before any organ-specific system fails.
Q10Hard
Which combination correctly represents the path of oxygen from atmosphere to body cells?
ATrachea → Bronchi → Alveoli → Blood → Cells
BBronchi → Trachea → Alveoli → Blood → Cells
CTrachea → Alveoli → Bronchi → Blood → Cells
DTrachea → Bronchi → Blood → Alveoli → Cells
Correct Answer
A — Trachea → Bronchi → Alveoli → Blood → Cells. Air travels through the trachea, branches into bronchi, reaches alveoli where gas exchange occurs, then oxygen diffuses into blood and is carried to cells.

True / False
8 statements
TF1Hard
Transpiration is essential because it directly provides energy for photosynthesis.
Answer
False. Transpiration is essential for water and mineral transport, not as a direct energy source for photosynthesis. Light energy is the energy source for photosynthesis.
TF2Hard
All absorbed water in plants is used in photosynthesis.
Answer
False. Only a tiny fraction (less than 2%) of absorbed water is actually used in photosynthesis. The vast majority (~98%) is lost through transpiration.
TF3Hard
The blood entering the lungs through the pulmonary artery is deoxygenated.
Answer
True. The pulmonary artery is a classic exception — it is an artery that carries deoxygenated blood from the right ventricle to the lungs for oxygenation.
TF4Hard
Food digestion is completed entirely in the stomach.
Answer
False. The stomach only partially digests proteins. Most digestion — of carbohydrates, fats, and remaining proteins — is completed in the small intestine with the help of bile and pancreatic enzymes.
TF5Hard
Nephrons help maintain water balance in the body.
Answer
True. Nephrons regulate water reabsorption from the filtrate, directly controlling the concentration and volume of urine, thereby maintaining the body's water balance (osmoregulation).
TF6Very Hard
Arteries always carry oxygenated blood.
Answer
False. The pulmonary artery (right ventricle → lungs) carries deoxygenated blood. Arteries are defined by carrying blood away from the heart, not by oxygen content.
TF7Hard
Respiration and breathing are the same process.
Answer
False. Breathing is the physical intake/expulsion of air (external process). Respiration is the biochemical process inside cells that oxidises glucose to release ATP energy — they are distinct processes.
TF8Hard
The septum in the human heart increases efficiency of oxygen transport.
Answer
True. The septum completely separates oxygenated (left side) from deoxygenated (right side) blood, enabling double circulation and ensuring tissues receive fully oxygenated blood.

Challenge Questions
5 olympiad-level
C1Olympiad
Why would a plant wilt even when there is sufficient water in the soil?
Model Answer
Rate of transpiration exceeds rate of water absorption. If environmental conditions (high temperature, low humidity, strong wind) cause rapid water loss through stomata faster than roots can absorb it, the plant cells lose turgor pressure and wilt — even with abundant soil water.
C2Olympiad
A person has normal digestion and absorption but cannot transport oxygen efficiently. Which blood component is most likely affected?
Model Answer
RBCs / Haemoglobin. Red blood cells contain haemoglobin, the protein that binds and transports oxygen. Deficiency (anaemia) or structural abnormality of haemoglobin (sickle cell) directly impairs oxygen delivery despite normal digestion.
C3Olympiad
Why can a fish not survive on land despite having access to atmospheric oxygen?
Model Answer
Gills collapse in air and cannot exchange gases. Fish gills have thin lamellae that only function when supported and separated by water. In air, the lamellae stick together (surface tension collapses the structure), eliminating the large surface area needed for gas exchange. The fish suffocates despite being surrounded by oxygen.
C4Olympiad
A blockage occurs in the renal artery. Which process of urine formation will be affected first?
Model Answer
Ultrafiltration in the nephrons (glomerular filtration). Ultrafiltration depends entirely on high blood pressure in the glomerulus (supplied by the renal artery). A blockage reduces blood flow and pressure, immediately halting filtration — the very first step. Tubular reabsorption and secretion cannot occur without a filtrate to process.
C5Olympiad
Why is double circulation more efficient than single circulation?
Model Answer
Complete separation + maintained high pressure. In double circulation, blood passes through the heart twice per circuit: once for oxygenation (pulmonary circuit) and once for delivery (systemic circuit). This (1) prevents any mixing of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood, and (2) allows the left ventricle to generate sufficient pressure for systemic delivery — resulting in far more efficient oxygen transport to tissues.
